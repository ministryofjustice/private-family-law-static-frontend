What to do next

Mediation may be the best way for you to make child arrangements. 

Mediation is not relationship counselling.

Family mediation is a process in which an independent, professionally trained mediator helps you and the other parent or carer work out your child arrangements. The mediator is a professional who will work with you to help you make decisions based on your child's best interests. They listen to both sides and take a neutral approach.​

​

Mediation can also be helpful if you're updating your existing child arrangements. You may find it less stressful than going to court.​

​

The mediator will help you find a solution which works for you both and will explain what needs to happen to make an agreement between you legally binding.​

You can attend mediation sessions in person or remotely (for example by video or phone). You do not have to be in the same room or call as your ex-partner.  

Mediation:

  • is usually cheaper and quicker than going to court​
  • is flexible to your needs​
  • can be partly or fully funded by government voucher scheme​
  • lets you have control over the outcome 
How mediation works

How it works:

  1. Find a mediator.
  2. Book an initial meeting, known as a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM). 
  3. Child-related mediation typically takes 2-4 sessions.​
  4. Get an agreement in writing. 

Get up to £500 towards the cost of mediation​

You can usually get a voucher worth up to £500 to pay for family mediation, no matter what you earn. The voucher usually pays for at least 2 mediation sessions.​

​

How to get a mediation voucher​

Ask an accredited mediator to apply for the voucher. You do not need to pay upfront for any sessions the voucher covers.

Other options

Collaborative law 

In this approach you and the other parent each hire a lawyer then you and the lawyers all meet to negotiate face-to-face.

Collaborative law can be a good choice if you can communicate with the other parent but have complex legal issues to resolve. This approach works best if you trust the other parent and can co-operate with them.​

Collaborative law:

  • lets you set the agenda and feel supported​
  • can be expensive​
  • may be quicker and less stressful than court​
  • lets you have some control over the outcome​
How collaborative law works

How it works:

  1. Hire a lawyer.
  2. Meet with your own lawyer to discuss your needs.
  3. Attend as many meetings as you need to with the other parent and their lawyer.
  4. Sign an agreement.

Lawyer negotiation

When you resolve your child arrangements using lawyer negotiation, you hire a lawyer to negotiate arrangements for you. The type of lawyer you use is called a solicitor. If the other parent also uses a lawyer then the two lawyers will speak directly to one another. This approach means that you don't have to deal directly with the other parent if you don’t want to. 

You can still hire a lawyer to negotiate on your behalf even if the other parent chooses not to use one. 

Lawyer negotiation can be a good option if your relationship is difficult and you'd prefer not to meet, or if you don't trust the other parent.

Lawyer negotiation:

  • means you don’t have to deal with the other parent directly
  • is usually quicker and less stressful than going to court
  • can be expensive
How lawyer negotiation works

How it works:

  1. Hire a lawyer.
  2. Meet with your lawyer to explain what you want to achieve.
  3. Continue working with your lawyer as they negotiate with the other parent.
  4. Sign an agreement.
