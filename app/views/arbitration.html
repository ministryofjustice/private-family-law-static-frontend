Arbitration

You could consider family arbitration to resolve your child arrangements.

In family arbitration, you and the other person choose a qualified, impartial professional to hear both of your views. They will then make a decision that you both have to agree to follow.

Arbitration:

  • is usually quicker and more flexible than going to court​
  • is expensive
Find out more about arbitration

The other parent will need to agree to arbitration and you'll need to be able to agree on which issues it is you want to focus on.

You get to choose how you want to participate in arbitration. For example, you can decide for the case to be dealt with on paper, or you can attend telephone or in-person meetings with the arbitrator. This approach means that you don't have to deal directly with the other parent if you don't want to.

You can also hire a lawyer to support you as part of the process. The type of lawyer you use is called a solicitor.

 

The agreement reached will not be legally binding. You can ask your lawyer to submit it to court to make it legally binding if that is what you want.

How it works:

  1. Both of you have to agree to arbitration.
  2. Choose an arbitrator
  3. Both of you complete an application form describing the issues you want to fix and whether you want to attend sessions or handle things with paperwork.​
  4. Take part in arbitration in the way you want, such as by phone or on paper.
  5. Get a decision in writing which you must both follow.

Other options

Lawyer negotiation

When you make or change your child arrangements using lawyer negotiation, you hire a lawyer to negotiate arrangements for you. This means that you don't have to deal directly with the other parent if you don't want to.

Lawyer negotiation:

  • is usually quicker and less stressful than going to court
  • can be expensive
How lawyer negotiation works

Lawyer negotiation can be a good option if your relationship is difficult and you'd prefer not to meet, or if you don't trust the other parent.

You can still hire a lawyer to negotiate on your behalf even if the other parent chooses not to use one. The type of lawyer you use is called a solicitor. Often the other parent will also use a lawyer and the two lawyers will speak directly to one another.

The agreement reached will not be legally binding. You can ask your lawyer to submit it to court to make it legally binding if that is what you want.

How it works:

  1. Hire a lawyer.
  2. Meet with your lawyer to explain what you want to achieve.
  3. Continue working with your lawyer as they negotiate with the other parent.
  4. Sign an agreement.

Going to court

You'll need to go to court if you've tried other suitable options and still can't agree arrangements, or you're worried about the welfare of you or your children.

Court:

  • can take a long time
  • can be expensive and stressful
  • often takes more than one court session to get a resolution
  • results in a legal judgement that can be enforced
How court works

How it works:

  1. Find out if you can get representation.
  2. You must attend an initial mediation meeting, known as a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM) before going to court, or complete an exemption form (opens in new tab).
  3. Apply to court
  4. Go to court
