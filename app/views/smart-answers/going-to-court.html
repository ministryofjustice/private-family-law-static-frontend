Going to court

You'll need to go to court if you've tried other suitable options and still can't agree arrangements, or you're worried about the welfare of you or your children.

Court:

  • can take a long time
  • can be expensive and stressful
  • often takes more than one court session to get a resolution
  • results in a legal judgement that can be enforced

How it works:

  1. Find out if you can get representation.
  2. You must attend an initial mediation meeting, known as a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM) before going to court, or complete an exemption form (opens in new tab).
  3. Apply to court
  4. Go to court

Other options

Arbitration

In family arbitration, you and the other person choose a qualified, impartial professional to hear both of your views. They will then make a decision that you both have agreed to.

The other parent will need to agree to arbitration and you’ll need to describe which issues it is you want to resolve.

You get to choose how you want to participate in arbitration. For example, you can decide for the case to be dealt with on paper, or you can attend telephone or in-person meetings with the arbitrator.

This approach means that you don't have to deal directly with the other parent if you don’t want to.

 

You can also hire a lawyer to support you as part of the process. The type of lawyer you use is called a solicitor.

Arbitration:

  • needs you to be able to agree what the issues are to resolve
  • is usually quicker and more flexible than going to court
  • is expensive

The agreement reached will not be legally binding. You can ask your lawyer to submit it to court to make it legally binding if that is what you want. 

Find out more about arbitration

How it works:

  1. Both of you have to agree to arbitration.
  2. Choose an arbitrator
  3. Both of you complete an application form describing the issues you want to fix and whether you want to attend sessions or handle things with paperwork.
  4. Take part in arbitration in the way you want, such as by phone or on paper.
  5. Get a decision in writing which you must both follow.

Lawyer negotiation

When you resolve your child arrangements using lawyer negotiation, you hire a lawyer to negotiate arrangements for you. The type of lawyer you use is called a solicitor. Often the other parent will also use a lawyer and the two lawyers will speak directly to one another. This approach means that you don't have to deal directly with the other parent if you don’t want to. 

You can still hire a lawyer to negotiate on your behalf even if the other parent chooses not to use one.

Lawyer negotiation can be a good option if your relationship is difficult and you'd prefer not to meet, or if you don’t trust the other parent. It can be expensive. It may be quicker and less stressful than court.

Lawyer negotiation:

  • means you don’t have to deal with the other parent directly
  • is usually quicker and less stressful than going to court
  • can be expensive

The agreement reached will not be legally binding. You can ask your lawyer to submit it to court to make it legally binding if that is what you want.

Find out more about lawyer negotiation

How it works:

  1. Hire a lawyer.
  2. Meet with your lawyer to explain what you want to achieve.
  3. Continue working with your lawyer as they negotiate with the other parent.
  4. Sign an agreement.
